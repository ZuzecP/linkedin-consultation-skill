## 1. The "First Impression" Framework: URL, Photo, and Headline

> Your profile starts working before the recruiter even clicks on it. It starts in the search results. Now with AI more than ever before.

**The URL**
If your link looks like `linkedin.com/in/john-doe-7a921b123`, you're telling the world you're a novice or that you do not care. Clean it up. Use name-surname. If your name is very common, add a distinguisher.
- Pro tip: `name-surname-role` is better for SEO/GEO and AI/automated recruitment tools, and also looks professional in an email signature.

**The Photo**
This isn't Instagram, but it isn't a passport office either. Go for an approachable headshot, chest-up, with a clean background.
- Pro tip: Look like the person the recruiter is going to meet on the video call. If your photo is from 2018, you're going to have an awkward first 30 seconds of the interview.

**The Headline**
This is your punchline. It's also the primary text the AI-driven LinkedIn Recruiter tool scrapes for keywords. A multiple-lines-long headline will not always show — always prioritise and optimise for the greatest impact.

> 🛑 Reality Check: The average recruiter spends only a couple of seconds on your headline before deciding to scroll down or bounce. Tell the world what you do, not what you want or what you believe — be specific, use keywords, symbols and emojis for better legibility.

**Good vs. Great Headlines:**

| Role | Good (Too Generic) | Great (Impact + Keywords) |
|---|---|---|
| Developer / Engineer | Full Stack Developer | Senior Full Stack Engineer |
| | Application Developer | Typescript Software Engineer |
| Product | Product Manager | Senior Product Manager @ B2B SaaS |

### Bonus points
**The Cover Picture:** Not utilising this space is a wasted opportunity. Consider it a free ad banner.
- Don't go generic: avoid stock photos of "generic office building" or "person typing on a laptop."
- Showcase your skills/stack, or use it as social proof — a photo of you speaking at a conference, leading a workshop, or leading a presentation immediately signals your value.
- You can include a short text overlay: your value proposition, certifications (AWS / Google Certified), or role + impact (doing ABC for platform used by XYZ).

**The Location:** Accuracy is essential — does your location match the area you are actually trying to be found for? Crucial for AI-driven location filters.

## 2. The Narrative: About & Featured Sections

**The About Section**
Help people understand your career path. Careers are becoming less and less linear — draw them a clear picture on how to read your CV.

> The "About" section is where you tell the "Why" behind the "What." Don't just list skills — weave a narrative.

- **Optimise for attention:** Put the most important information first, then expand. Think of it as a Press Release opening.
- **Tone of voice:** Should be consistent across the whole profile. Here you can show your genuine self.
- **Focus on the future:** Outline what your next career move should be — including what you are definitely NOT interested in.
- **Transferrable skills:** Showcase the transferrable skills collected across various roles.
- **Personality markers:** You might mention your Gallup/CliftonStrengths or MBTI if relevant to your work style (e.g., "Strategic" or "Achiever"). It gives a hook for the first conversation with the recruiter/hiring manager.

**The Featured Section**
This is your portfolio. Select the crème de la crème of your work, activities, and projects.
- Dev → link GitHub and personal site
- Marketer → link a Medium article or case study
- HR → show a deck on "Building a Speak-up Culture"
- Public speaker → link to a post about the conference with photos

### Bonus points
- Aim for 3+ objects in the Featured carousel (prioritisation is key)
- 🤖 AI Sourcing Tip (2026): If your profile is just text, AI thinks you're an "average" candidate. If you have a Featured section with external links (Behance, GitHub, Medium), AI classifies you as an "Expert-Level" match. It's the difference between page 1 vs. page 10 of a search.

## 3. The "Meat": Experience that Actually Converts

Most people write their experience section like a job description. Recruiters already know what the role requires. They want to know what *you* did.

**The Golden Formula:** `[Action Verb] + [Quantifiable Result] + [Method/Tool]`
- ❌ Bad: "Responsible for managing the dev team."
- ✅ Great: "Led a cross-functional squad of 8 to reduce API latency by 40% using Node.js and Redis caching."

> Take it with a grain of salt. Not every sentence has to sound like that. Stay genuine and true to your tone of voice. But if you cringe a bit while reading your own description — GOOD. That is what we are aiming for.

**Formatting tips:**
- Break the text with other formats — optimise for attention. Make those first two lines stand out.
- Use LinkedIn's "chips" (media: links, images, documents). Rule of thumb: clean visual AND copy for each caption.
- Structure the description by agendas, projects, or areas of expertise.
- Use dividers — special characters and symbols work great, not just emojis.

### Bonus points
**Handling Gaps and Freelancing:**
- Don't hide career breaks. Use the "Career Break" feature for "Health and well-being" or "Caregiving." It shows emotional intelligence and transparency.
- If it's only a month or two between jobs, skip it — it would only prolong your CV unnecessarily.
- For freelancing, group it under one heading and list notable projects underneath.

> 🛑 Reality Check: Recruiters actually click on the media links in your experience section. If you mention a "successful product launch," attach the press release or a screenshot of the UI. It proves you aren't inflating your resume.

## 4. Skills & The Algorithm: The Priority Game

LinkedIn allows you to list dozens of skills, but the **top 3 are the only ones that truly matter for the algorithm.**

If you are applying for a "Senior Automations" role but your top skills are "Public Speaking" and "Microsoft Word," the ATS will rank you lower.

- **Reorder them:** Drag your most relevant technical skills and expertise to the top.
- **Assign them:** Attach specific skills to specific roles in your experience section. This creates a "skill map" that AI tools and recruiters can use.

**Skills feature breakdown:**
- **Skills section** — main storage for all skills across your career. After reaching a certain number, skills are sorted into categories.
- **Top Skills** — select and prioritise the 5 top skills to help land the target role. This is the only opportunity to showcase skills "before the fold."
- **Experience / role-related Skills** — select the right set of skills for each role, and prioritise.

### Bonus points
- **Endorsements:** Nurture your network, endorse colleagues for skills you genuinely valued.

> 🛑 Reality Check: Having 500+ connections isn't just a vanity metric. It's a signal to recruiters that you are "plugged in." 20 mutual connections = 5x more likely to trust your profile.

## 5. The Recommendations

> Treat the recommendation section like your peer performance review — generic questions lead only to generic feedback.

**Framework for requesting a recommendation:**
1. **Set the scene / context:** Remind them of a specific project, initiative, or timeframe you worked together.
2. **Introduce the actors / give them options:** Ask them to focus on specific skills and/or outcomes. [By doing X we achieved Y]
3. **Always give them a way to say no** — or that they do not have to do it right away.
4. **Ease the pressure:** Provide a little draft as a "starting point" to save their time.

In one sentence: Ask for a specific recommendation. "Hey, could you write 3 sentences about how I handled the migration project last Q3?" **Specificity = Credibility.**

### Bonus points
- **Education & Licenses:** Use logos. Keep certifications updated.
- **Volunteering:** Mentoring junior devs or volunteering for a non-profit tells recruiters you're a human, not just a keyboard-masher.
- **Languages:** Well-defined languages including proficiency level might be the reason your profile makes the cut in a recruiter's search.

> 🛑 Reality Check: 60% of LinkedIn users use the platform from mobile. Recruiters sometimes review profiles from their phones too. Optimise for mobile view — especially your cover picture (the profile pic overlaps more of the cover on mobile than on desktop).

## 🚀 The Final Checklist

Before you hit 'Save' and start applying:

- [ ] **Clean URL:** Does it have my name (and a keyword)?
- [ ] **Location:** Is it set to where I want to work (or "Remote")?
- [ ] **Headline:** Does it have at least 3 searchable keywords? (No "Ninjas"!)
- [ ] **About Section:** Does it include my "Top Skills" and a bit of personality?
- [ ] **Experience:** Is every bullet point an "Action-Result" statement?
- [ ] **Skills:** Are my top 3-5 skills perfectly aligned with my target role?
- [ ] **Featured Section:** Is there at least one link to a real-world project?

You've got the talent. Now make sure the recruiter (and the robot) can actually find it.
